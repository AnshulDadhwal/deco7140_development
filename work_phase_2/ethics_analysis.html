Ethics Analysis

Potential Harms

The newsletter subscription feature poses multiple risks:

  • Privacy violation: Collecting sexuality, income, and credit card data exceeds normal expectations for a newsletter sign-up, exposing users to potential misuse or profiling.
  • Discrimination and exclusion: Sensitive data could be repurposed, leading to stereotyping or targeted exploitation.
  • Trust erosion: Communities already cautious about data collection (as shown in user stories where Chen and Luca reviewed plug-ins and data usage before subscribing) may avoid engaging with the website altogether.
  • Security risks: If breached, the dataset could cause financial harm, outing of sexuality or gender, or reputational damage to users.

ACS Values and Principles

Harm 1: Privacy Violation

Relevant Principles:
Trustworthiness (2.2d): Respect privacy, confidentiality, and integrity of personal information.
Respect for Others (2.3.1a & f): Minimise harm and mitigate risks associated with their work.

Ethical Solution: Only essential details (e.g., name and email) should be collected. Sensitive data should be avoided or made optional with clear explanations and informed consent.

Harm 2: Discrimination and Exclusion

Relevant Principles:
Respect for Others (2.3.1c & d): Requires impartiality, fairness, and inclusivity in system design.
Respect for the Profession (2.3.2c): Stresses defending the public interest and enhancing quality of life.

Ethical Solution: Remove mandatory fields that could lead to exclusion, and ensure inclusivity by designing sign-up processes that treat all users equitably.

Harm 3: Trust Erosion

Relevant Principles:
Honesty (2.1a & b): Requires transparency and openness about how data is used.
Trustworthiness (2.2a & b): Requires accountability, integrity, and avoidance of undue influence.

Ethical Solution: Provide transparent, plain-language explanations of why data is collected and how it is used, ensuring users can make informed choices.

Harm 4: Security Risks

Relevant Principles:
Trustworthiness (2.2h): Requires developing robust, secure, and user-friendly systems.
Respect for Others (2.3.1a & f): Requires minimising harm and mitigating risks.

Ethical Solution: If sensitive data must be collected, implement strong encryption, limit retention, and ensure compliance with best security practices; ideally, redesign the system to reduce data collection altogether.

Navigating Tensions

The client’s funder expects detailed demographic data, but users expect privacy and safe participation. This creates a tension between client obligations and ethical design. Options include:

  1. Propose a privacy-preserving version of the plug-in where only essential fields are required, and other fields are clearly optional.
  2. Offer alternative data collection methods (e.g., anonymous, aggregated surveys) that still give funders insights without individual profiling.
  3. Provide user control and consent, allowing opt-in for specific categories of data.

By presenting these options, the design team shows respect for the client’s goals while prioritising community trust and aligning with ACS’s principle of public interest.
